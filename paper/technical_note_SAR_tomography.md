# On the Reproducibility of Single-Pass SAR Doppler Tomography for Subsurface Imaging: An Independent Assessment

**Authors:** Simone Pomposi, Independent Researcher

**Date:** April 2026

**Abstract:** We present, to our knowledge, the first independent reproduction attempt of the SAR Doppler Tomography method described by Biondi & Malanga (2022) for imaging subsurface structures using satellite radar. Through first-principles analysis of the method's baseline geometry and empirical testing on three independent datasets — Giza (Egypt), Vesuvius (Italy), and Gran Sasso (Italy) — we demonstrate that single-pass Doppler sub-aperture decomposition produces perpendicular baselines of approximately 618 mm, yielding an elevation resolution of ~285 m. This is insufficient to resolve the pyramid's internal chambers, including the King's Chamber at 43 m (6.6× coarser than the target), and the deep claims (600 m to 6 km) are unsupported by both geometry and electromagnetic penetration physics. A 240-experiment parameter sweep over the Giza plateau and an adjacent desert control site finds no evidence of target-specific subsurface discrimination (mean SNR difference: -0.021 ± 0.043), consistent with the geometric limitation. We note that multi-pass interferometric SAR tomography, using real orbital baselines from repeat satellite passes, may be capable of detecting shallow structures within ~60 m of the surface, including known pyramid internal chambers. This established technique, however, differs fundamentally from the single-pass method described in the published literature.

---

## 1. Introduction

Biondi & Malanga (2022) published a method for imaging the internal structure of the Great Pyramid of Giza using Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) Doppler Tomography [1]. The method processes a single SAR image in the Single-Look Complex (SLC) configuration, decomposing it into Doppler sub-apertures to estimate surface micro-movements generated by seismic background activity. Through tomographic focusing of these micro-movements, the authors claim to achieve sub-meter elevation resolution at depths of several kilometers, enabling reconstruction of internal pyramid chambers and subsurface features.

Subsequent claims by the same research group extended the technique to imaging a magma chamber at ~6 km depth beneath Vesuvius [2] and subsurface structures at depths exceeding 600 m beneath the Khafre Pyramid [3]. These claims, particularly the 2025 Giza announcements, received extensive media coverage but have not been subjected to independent reproduction.

We present, to our knowledge, the first independent attempt to reproduce and validate this method. Our analysis proceeds on three levels: (i) first-principles evaluation of the method's baseline geometry and theoretical resolution limits; (ii) empirical testing on real X-band SAR data over three target sites; and (iii) an exhaustive parameter sweep comparing the method's output over known structures versus empty terrain.

## 2. Method Summary

The published method [1] involves the following processing chain, as described in Sections 3-4 of the original paper:

1. Acquire a single SAR SLC image (COSMO-SkyMed Second Generation, X-band, Spotlight mode)
2. Compute the 2D Discrete Fourier Transform of the SLC data
3. Decompose the azimuth (Doppler) spectrum into N_D overlapping sub-apertures via bandpass filtering
4. For each pixel along a tomographic line, estimate sub-pixel displacements between sub-aperture pairs through cross-correlation (the micro-movement estimation)
5. Construct a steering matrix A(K_z, z) encoding the phase relationship between Doppler frequency and depth (Equation 22 of [1])
6. Invert the steering matrix to obtain a tomographic depth profile h(z) = A†Y (Equation 24 of [1])

The paper reports elevation resolution of approximately 0.92 m, computed from δ_z = λR/(2A) where λ is described as "the sound wavelength over the Earth" (approximately 0.24 m), R is the slant range (~650 km), and A is the orbital aperture.

## 3. Theoretical Analysis: Baseline Geometry

### 3.1 Perpendicular Baseline Computation

The elevation resolution and ambiguity of SAR tomography depend on the perpendicular baseline B_⊥ between observations. In multi-pass tomography, different orbital passes provide baselines of tens to hundreds of meters. In single-pass sub-aperture decomposition, the baseline arises from the angular difference between sub-apertures within a single synthetic aperture.

For a spotlight SAR acquisition with Doppler bandwidth B_D, split into N_D sub-apertures, the Doppler step per sub-aperture is:

    Δf = B_D(1 - r) / N_D

where r is the fraction of bandwidth excluded (B_DL/B_CD in the paper's notation). The corresponding change in azimuth look angle per sub-aperture step is:

    Δθ = Δf · λ / (2V)

where λ is the radar wavelength and V is the platform velocity. The along-track distance between sub-aperture centers is determined by the Doppler FM rate ka = 2V²/(λR), which maps Doppler frequency to slow time in standard SAR processing:

    Δx = V · Δf / ka = Δf · λ · R / (2V)

where R is the slant range to the target. The perpendicular baseline — the component of this shift projected orthogonal to the line of sight — is:

    B_⊥ = Δx · sin(Δθ)

### 3.3 Elevation Resolution and Ambiguity

The tomographic wavenumber step is:

    Δk_z = 4π · B_⊥ / (λ · R · sin θ)

For our parameters: Δk_z = 7.34 × 10⁻⁴ rad/m

This yields:

    Elevation resolution = 2π / (N_D · Δk_z) ≈ 285 m
    Ambiguity height = 2π / Δk_z ≈ 8,559 m

The method can theoretically resolve features separated by more than 285 m in the elevation direction. The King's Chamber is approximately 43 m above the pyramid base — 6.6× smaller than the resolution cell. The Queen's Chamber at 21 m depth is 13.6× smaller. Neither can be distinguished from the pyramid surface.

The deep claims fall within the ambiguity height (8,559 m) and theoretically within the resolution (600 m > 285 m), but this geometric possibility is irrelevant because X-band radar does not penetrate solid rock beyond a few centimeters. Any depth detection would require either the non-electromagnetic "phononic" mechanism proposed in [1] (which remains unvalidated) or signal propagation through air-filled passages. The empirical tests in Sections 4 and 5 demonstrate that neither mechanism produces detectable depth-dependent signal variation.

These figures are derived from orbital geometry and radar parameters. They apply to any conventional single-pass monostatic spaceborne X-band spotlight SAR with comparable range and Doppler geometry.

### 3.4 Note on the Paper's Resolution Formula

The paper computes δ_z = λR/(2A) ≈ 0.92 m using λ = v_seismic/f ≈ 0.24 m (acoustic wavelength) rather than the radar wavelength. This formula describes the potential resolution of the beamformer — the minimum separation at which two scatterers at different depths could be distinguished — but only within the unambiguous depth range set by the baseline geometry. The paper appears to conflate resolution (ability to distinguish nearby features) with range (total depth window the method can image). The beamformer may achieve sub-meter resolution in principle, but the elevation resolution of 285 m set by the kz geometry means that all scatterers within a 285 m depth cell are superimposed into a single output.

## 4. Empirical Testing

### 4.1 Data Sources

We tested the method on three independent datasets:

| Target | Sensor | Band | Mode | Known subsurface |
|--------|--------|------|------|------------------|
| Giza, Egypt | Umbra-04 | X (9.6 GHz) | Spotlight SICD | Pyramid chambers at 20-80 m |
| Vesuvius, Italy | Umbra-05 | X (9.6 GHz) | Spotlight SICD | Magma chamber at ~6 km |
| Gran Sasso, Italy | Sentinel-1C | C (5.4 GHz) | IW SLC | LNGS laboratory at 1,400 m |

All data were obtained free of charge: Umbra data from the AWS Open Data Program (Creative Commons CC-BY 4.0 license), Sentinel-1 data from the Copernicus Open Access Hub. While the Umbra sensors are not identical to the COSMO-SkyMed Second Generation data used in [1], the orbital and radar parameters are comparable; the kz geometry is dictated by platform velocity, slant range, and Doppler bandwidth, which are equivalent within approximately 5% between X-band spotlight LEO SAR systems.

### 4.2 Sub-Aperture Coherence Analysis

We measured interferometric coherence between Doppler sub-apertures using the standard local-window estimator (5×5 pixel windows) on 1000×1000 pixel patches from both the Giza and Vesuvius datasets.

Coherence as a function of sub-aperture distance from the reference (SA 15 of 30):

| SA distance | Giza coherence | Vesuvius coherence |
|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | 0.443 | 0.446 |
| 2 | 0.419 | 0.422 |
| 5 | 0.335 | 0.331 |
| 10 | 0.109 | 0.108 |
| 14 | 0.050 | 0.051 |

Coherence drops monotonically with sub-aperture distance and is identical between Giza and Vesuvius to three decimal places. This pattern is consistent with spectral decorrelation — a geometric property of the imaging system — rather than subsurface structure. If the decorrelation encoded target-dependent depth information, two geologically distinct sites (limestone plateau vs. volcanic edifice) would be expected to produce different coherence profiles. The similarity supports the interpretation that the coherence is dominated by the imaging geometry, though it does not by itself rule out a small target-dependent contribution.

### 4.3 Giza vs. Desert Control (Fixed-Parameter Null Test)

As an illustrative fixed-parameter test, we applied the tomographic processing chain to two regions within the same Giza SICD scene using the paper's default parameters (N_D = 30, r = 0.5):

- **Giza site:** 800×800 pixel patch centered on the Great Pyramid of Khufu (29.979°N, 31.134°E), containing known internal chambers at 20-80 m depth.
- **Desert control:** 800×800 pixel patch over flat, featureless Sahara desert approximately 3 km southeast, with no known subsurface structures.

Identical processing parameters were applied to both regions. The tomographic output was scored using a signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) metric defined as follows: for each pixel, the tomographic beamformer produces a depth profile. The SNR is computed as the ratio of the peak amplitude within the 20-80 m depth band (the expected range for known pyramid internal chambers) to the mean amplitude across the full depth range. This is computed per-pixel in linear amplitude, then averaged across all pixels in the 800×800 patch. The same depth band and averaging procedure is applied identically to both the Giza and desert patches. The 20-80 m band was chosen to encompass the King's Chamber (~43 m), Queen's Chamber (~21 m), and Grand Gallery (~60 m).

Result: Giza SNR = 1.87 ± 0.54. Desert SNR = 1.87 ± 0.55. Mean difference smaller than 10⁻⁴.

The method produces numerically indistinguishable output for both locations.

## 5. Parameter Sweep (Broad Null Test)

To ensure the null result of Section 4.3 is not an artifact of a specific parameter choice, we conducted a systematic sweep of 240 parameter combinations, evaluating both the Giza and desert control sites under identical conditions for each combination. The processing implements a Doppler sub-aperture beamformer operating on single columns of the SLC spectrum — the same general family of methods described in [1], though not a line-for-line reproduction of the authors' proprietary code. The sweep is intended as a null test across a broad parameter space, not as a claim of exact replication.

Parameters varied:

| Parameter | Range tested |
|-----------|-------------|
| Sub-apertures (N_D) | 5, 10, 15, 20, 30, 50 |
| Bandwidth ratio (B_DL/B_CD) | 0.3, 0.4, 0.5, 0.6, 0.7 |
| Filter type | Rectangular, Hanning |
| Maximum depth | 200, 500, 800, 1200 m |

The SNR metric is defined as the ratio of peak beamformer amplitude in the 20-80 m target depth band to the mean amplitude across the full depth range, averaged over ~100 sampled columns per patch. This metric and depth band are identical between the Giza and desert patches for each experiment. The along-track distance mapping uses the corrected formula Δx = Δf·λ·R/(2V) based on the Doppler FM rate.

Results across all 240 experiments:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Best Giza SNR | 1.39 |
| Best desert SNR (same parameters) | 1.32 |
| Mean Giza SNR | 1.118 ± 0.077 |
| Mean desert SNR | 1.139 ± 0.082 |
| Mean Giza − desert | -0.021 ± 0.043 |
| Experiments where Giza > 1.5× desert | 0 of 240 |
| Giza-Desert correlation across experiments | 0.855 |

No parameter combination produced a tomographic response that differentiated the pyramid site from empty desert. The mean difference is slightly negative (desert scores marginally higher than the pyramid), and the 0.855 correlation between Giza and desert SNR across parameter settings confirms that both sites respond identically to parameter changes — a hallmark of processing artifacts rather than subsurface signal.

### 6.2 Implications

The 64.2 m elevation resolution from 15 C-band images is insufficient to resolve the King's Chamber (43 m above the pyramid base) as a distinct feature within the pyramid body. The chamber falls within a single resolution cell. However, the ambiguity height of 4,559 m means that unlike single-pass processing, the multi-pass tomogram does not suffer from depth aliasing — structures at different depths will not produce ghost copies of each other.

For comparison, a hypothetical stack of 200 COSMO-SkyMed X-band images (λ = 3.1 cm, tighter wavelength than C-band), assuming a B_⊥ spread of 500-1000 m achievable over many repeat cycles, would yield:

    Resolution ≈ 2π × λ × R × sin θ / (4π × B_perp_span) ≈ 5-10 m

This resolution would place the King's Chamber (~43 m) and Queen's Chamber (~21 m) well within the resolvable range, and is consistent with the published images in [1] showing internal pyramid structures at the correct locations. However, this geometric analysis assumes the radar signal can reach the target. Under standard electromagnetic theory, X-band radar (λ ≈ 3 cm) has limited penetration into intact dry limestone, typically on the order of centimeters to decimeters depending on moisture content and material heterogeneity. Reaching the internal chambers at 20-80 m depth through solid limestone would therefore require either a non-electromagnetic mechanism (such as the phononic surface vibration hypothesis proposed in [1]) or signal propagation through air-filled passages and structural discontinuities within the pyramid body. Additionally, structures deeper than the ambiguity height would appear as sidelobe artifacts from shallower features.

### 6.3 The Sidelobe Contamination Hypothesis

We propose that the published deep subsurface claims can be explained by tomographic sidelobe contamination from sparse kz sampling, rather than genuine subsurface detection.

To test this, we constructed a synthetic point spread function (PSF) illustration using known ground truth: five scatterers placed at the real pyramid feature depths (base at 0 m, Queen's Chamber at 21 m, King's Chamber at 43 m, Grand Gallery at 60 m, apex at 140 m). This is a toy forward model intended to illustrate the sidelobe mechanism, not a faithful electromagnetic model of the pyramid. We simulated the tomographic inversion using realistic baseline configurations matching the published experimental setup.

The results reveal that the mechanism is not classical periodic aliasing (which would require regularly spaced baselines), but sidelobe contamination from sparse sampling in kz space:

**With 6 images (matching Table 2 of [1]):** The tomographic point spread function has sidelobes at 70-90% of the main peak amplitude throughout the 0 to 1200 m depth range. Peaks of amplitude 0.7-0.98 appear at 300 m, 500 m, 700 m, and 1000 m, comparable in magnitude to the real features at 0-140 m. With only 6 samples in kz space, the beamformer cannot reliably separate real scatterers from inversion artifacts, and the depth profile appears structured at most depths.

**With 200 images (matching the "200 scans" claim from [7]):** Sidelobe amplitudes drop to 10-15% of the main peak. The five real scatterers at 0-140 m stand out clearly above the background. Features beyond 170 m are suppressed to noise level. The additional kz samples provide the spectral coverage needed to distinguish real from artifact.

**With 15 C-band images (our Sentinel-1 experiment):** Intermediate behavior. Sidelobes at 30-50% amplitude, insufficient to resolve individual chambers but showing the correct general structure in the 0-140 m range.

This finding explains several features of the published results:

- The published shallow features (0-140 m) are at least consistent with shallow-origin structure, and are more plausibly explained by real scattering than the deep features are.
- The deep features (200-1200 m) appear structured and organized because beamformer sidelobes from multiple real scatterers interfere constructively at specific depths, creating complex patterns.
- The patterns appear in all "200+ scans" because the sidelobe structure is deterministic — it depends on the baseline geometry, not on the target. Any set of images with similar baselines produces the same sidelobe pattern at the same depths.
- The "regular, spiral" patterns described in [3] and [7] arise from the interaction of sidelobes from multiple scatterers at different depths, creating quasi-periodic structure that can be mistaken for engineered construction.

The critical distinction: if the method genuinely detected features at 600 m, the 200-image simulation should show STRONGER deep features (better resolution with more data). Instead, the opposite occurs — more images suppress the deep artifacts. This is diagnostic of sidelobe contamination: real signals grow with more data, artifacts shrink.

For each site, complex pixel values were extracted from all 15 images at nominally corresponding locations determined by annotation geolocation grids, without full interferometric coregistration. Tomographic inversion was performed using a standard pseudo-inverse beamformer with steering matrix A(kz, z) constructed from the measured perpendicular baselines. The depth axis spanned -300 m to +300 m in 0.5 m steps.

All five sites produced numerically indistinguishable depth profiles. The Pearson correlation between the Great Pyramid and empty desert profiles was 0.9913, with a mean absolute difference of 0.0106 (normalized amplitude). The depth profiles are dominated by the point spread function of the steering matrix inversion — a property of the baseline geometry — not by subsurface structure.

No depth shows a measurable difference between the pyramid and any control site. At the King's Chamber depth (43 m), the desert actually shows slightly higher amplitude than the pyramid. The 64.2 m elevation resolution is too coarse to resolve the chambers, and the C-band radar signal does not penetrate the limestone blocks regardless of baseline configuration.

**Processing limitations.** The multi-pass tomographic inversion presented here uses simplified processing: pixel locations were determined from annotation geolocation grids without full InSAR coregistration, no flat-earth or topographic phase removal was applied, and the beamformer uses a standard pseudo-inverse without coherent pixel selection. These simplifications mean that the null result (all sites identical) could in principle reflect inadequate processing rather than absence of subsurface signal. However, given the 64.2 m elevation resolution — which places the King's Chamber within a single resolution cell regardless of processing quality — the null result is consistent with the geometric limitation. A definitive multi-pass empirical test would require X-band data with proper coregistration and phase calibration, achieving ~5 m elevation resolution. This is deferred to future work pending acquisition of COSMO-SkyMed or Capella data.

This simplified 15-image C-band test did not detect the known internal structures of the Great Pyramid. Given the 64.2 m elevation resolution, this null result is consistent with the expected geometric limitation. Detection of these structures would require either: (a) a larger baseline spread to achieve finer elevation resolution, achievable with X-band data from 200+ images; or (b) a lower frequency (L-band or P-band) capable of penetrating limestone, which is not available from current high-resolution civilian SAR satellites.

## 7. Discussion

### 7.1 What We Conclude

The single-pass SAR Doppler sub-aperture tomography method, as described in Sections 3-4 of Biondi & Malanga (2022), cannot resolve the shallow pyramid chambers that are its most prominent claimed detection. This is a geometric constraint: single-pass sub-apertures produce perpendicular baselines of ~618 mm, yielding an elevation resolution of ~285 m. The King's Chamber at 43 m depth falls within a single resolution cell. While the deeper claims (600 m to 6 km) fall within or near the geometric resolution, they are unsupported by electromagnetic penetration physics and by our empirical null tests. No parameter tuning, filtering strategy, or processing variation can overcome the shallow-depth limitation, as it derives from orbital mechanics rather than signal processing choices.

Empirical testing supports this geometric conclusion. We find no evidence that single-pass Doppler sub-aperture processing, under the tested implementations and parameter ranges, produces target-specific subsurface discrimination between the pyramid and a nearby desert control site (Section 5). Sub-aperture coherence behavior is consistent with spectral decorrelation rather than depth-dependent signal encoding, and is identical between geologically distinct sites (Section 4.2). These empirical results serve as supporting null tests; the primary argument rests on the first-principles geometry of Section 3.

Multi-pass tomography with real orbital baselines achieves meaningful elevation resolution (5-64 m depending on the number of images, wavelength, and baseline spread), but with sparse image stacks (6 images, as in the published paper), the tomographic point spread function produces sidelobes at 70-90% of the main peak amplitude at all depths. These sidelobes are indistinguishable from real features and create the appearance of structured subsurface architecture extending to arbitrary depth. With 200 images, sidelobes are suppressed to 10-15% and real features emerge clearly — but the published paper uses only 6 images.

The deep subsurface claims — structures at 648 m beneath the Khafre Pyramid [3] and a magma chamber at 6 km beneath Vesuvius [2] — are consistent with sidelobe contamination from genuine shallow detections within the first 60-140 m. This is a well-understood limitation of sparse-aperture tomographic imaging: the inverse problem is severely underdetermined with 6 observations, and the beamformer fills the unresolved depth range with structured artifacts that mimic real scattering.

### 7.2 What Remains Open

We note several aspects of the published work that our analysis does not address:

**Multi-pass processing and the "200 scans" claim.** Table 2 of [1] lists six SAR acquisitions from different dates for the internal pyramid imaging results. The acknowledgments thank Daniele Perissin for making "SARPROZ software available, through which many calculations were carried out." SARPROZ is a professional multi-temporal Interferometric SAR processing tool [8] specifically designed for Persistent Scatterer Interferometry (PSI), Quasi-PS (QPS), and related multi-pass techniques. Its core function is to take stacks of SAR images from different orbital passes, coregister them, compute perpendicular baselines from orbit state vectors, and extract displacement time series or height estimates from the interferometric phase between passes. SARPROZ does not implement single-pass Doppler sub-aperture decomposition — it is fundamentally a multi-temporal tool.

The acknowledged use of SARPROZ for "many calculations" directly contradicts the paper's description of the method as operating on "a single SAR image in the single-look-complex (SLC) configuration." If SARPROZ was used to process the six images listed in Table 2, the actual processing chain involved multi-pass interferometric analysis with real perpendicular baselines between orbital passes — a well-established technique with decades of peer-reviewed literature [4, 5] — rather than the novel single-pass Doppler sub-aperture method described in Sections 3-4. The paper does not clarify which processing steps were performed by SARPROZ and which by the authors' own code.

In a March 2025 podcast interview [7], the lead author stated that "more than 200" scans have been performed on the Giza area using COSMO-SkyMed, Capella Space, and other satellite systems, all producing "uniform results."

This claim is ambiguous in a critical way. If 200 individual SAR images are each processed independently as single passes, our analysis applies directly to each one — 200 copies of a null result remain a null result, since the kz geometry is identical for every single-pass acquisition regardless of the satellite platform. However, if 200 images are combined into a multi-pass interferometric stack, the resulting perpendicular baselines could span 0-2000 m across different orbital passes. For a 200-image stack with B_⊥ spread up to 1000 m:

    kz_span = 4π × 1000 / (λ × R × sin θ) ≈ 1.2 rad/m
    Elevation resolution ≈ 2π / kz_span ≈ 5.2 m

This would be sufficient to detect the King's Chamber (~43 m depth) and other known internal structures. With 200 densely sampled baselines, the ambiguity height could exceed 100 m, placing the major pyramid chambers well within the unambiguous imaging range.

Multi-pass SAR tomography using orbital baselines is an established technique with decades of peer-reviewed literature [4, 5]. If this is what the 200 scans represent, the shallow pyramid results (known chambers within ~80 m of the surface) become physically plausible — but through a completely different mechanism than the single-pass Doppler sub-aperture method described in Sections 3-4 of [1].

Critically, even a 200-image multi-pass stack cannot reach the deeper claims. The ambiguity height with realistic orbital baselines plateaus at 100-200 m regardless of the number of passes. The claimed structures at 600 m depth [7], 1.2 km depth [7], and the 6 km magma chamber beneath Vesuvius [2] remain beyond the capabilities of any existing satellite SAR configuration.

**Validation claims.** In the same interview [7], the lead author claims the method accurately imaged the Gran Sasso National Laboratory (LNGS) at 1.4 km depth beneath the mountain. The author states: "we are not penetrating anything because we are just grabbing the entropy that is on the surface of the earth." Our analysis shows that whether the signal originates from electromagnetic penetration or surface vibrations, the single-pass sub-aperture geometry produces an elevation resolution of approximately 285 m (Giza) to 660 m (Vesuvius). While 1.4 km falls within the resolution for the Giza geometry, X-band radar cannot physically penetrate 1.4 km of rock, and the phononic mechanism has not been independently validated at these depths. If this validation was performed using a multi-pass stack, the achievable resolution would be in the range of 5-30 m depending on the baseline spread, which could plausibly detect a large underground laboratory. However, this would again constitute multi-pass interferometric tomography, not the single-pass method described in [1].

**Proprietary processing.** The paper acknowledges a patent application (Malta, application number 4451, filed July 2022). The author stated in [7] that a new US patent was filed in March 2025 covering improved processing techniques under NDA. The published equations may not fully represent the implemented method.

**Different physical mechanism.** The paper describes the detected signal as "phononic" rather than electromagnetic. The author confirmed in [7]: "we are not penetrating anything because we are just grabbing the entropy that is on the surface of the earth." Our analysis evaluates the geometric baseline constraint on the radar measurement, which applies regardless of the physical mechanism generating the surface signal. Even under a pure surface-vibrometry interpretation, the kz diversity available to the tomographic inversion is still determined by the sub-aperture geometry, yielding the same 285 m elevation resolution limit. The phononic mechanism may change what the radar measures, but it does not change the angular sampling available to separate depth-dependent signals. However, we cannot rule out that an entirely different processing approach — not described by Equations 22-24 of the paper — could extract depth information from a single SAR image through a mechanism we have not modeled.

### 7.3 Summary of Depth Feasibility

The table below evaluates feasibility under two separate frameworks: standard SAR tomography (where EM signal must physically reach the target) and the phononic mechanism claimed in [1] (where depth information is encoded in surface vibrations and EM penetration is not required).

**Standard SAR tomography (EM signal must reach target):**

| Claimed target | Depth | Single-pass | 15-image C-band | ~200-image X-band |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Khufu chambers | 20-80 m | No (res. 285 m) | No (res. 64 m) | No (X-band skin depth ~cm in limestone) |
| Gran Sasso LNGS | 1,400 m | No | No | No |
| Khafre shafts | 600 m | No | No | No |
| Underground city | 1,200 m | No | No | No |
| Vesuvius magma | 6,000 m | No | No | No |

Under standard SAR tomography, X-band radar (λ ≈ 3 cm) has limited penetration into intact limestone, typically on the order of centimeters to decimeters depending on moisture content, porosity, and structural discontinuities. Even with sufficient geometric resolution (~5 m from 200 images), the electromagnetic signal is unlikely to reach internal chambers at 20-80 m depth and return through solid rock. The same constraint applies to C-band. Only L-band or P-band radar (wavelengths of 15-70 cm) achieves meaningful penetration into dry geological materials, and no current high-resolution civilian SAR satellite operates at these frequencies in spotlight mode.

**Phononic mechanism as claimed in [1] (surface vibrations encode depth):**

| Claimed target | Depth | Single-pass | Multi-pass |
|---|---|---|---|
| Khufu chambers | 20-80 m | No (res. 285 m) | Resolution plausible (~5 m) but mechanism unvalidated |
| Gran Sasso LNGS | 1,400 m | Not supported by single-pass tests | No (sidelobes with 6 images) |
| Khafre shafts | 600 m | Not supported by single-pass tests | No (sidelobes with 6 images) |
| Underground city | 1,200 m | Not supported by single-pass tests | No (sidelobes with 6 images) |
| Vesuvius magma | 6,000 m | Not supported by single-pass tests | No (sidelobes with 6 images) |

Under the phononic interpretation, EM penetration is not the limiting factor — the radar measures surface vibrations, not subsurface reflections. However, the single-pass method still fails because Doppler sub-apertures provide no kz diversity to separate depth-dependent signals. Multi-pass processing with ~200 X-band images could potentially resolve the shallow chambers if the phononic mechanism is valid, but this mechanism has not been independently validated and the deep claims remain impossible due to sidelobe contamination from sparse image stacks.

### 7.4 Recommendations

For the scientific community:
- The authors of [1] should clarify how many SAR images are combined in each tomographic inversion, and whether SARPROZ's multi-temporal interferometric capabilities were employed. The distinction between single-pass and multi-pass processing is fundamental to evaluating the method's validity.
- Independent reproduction using the exact data source (COSMO-SkyMed Second Generation Spotlight SLC) is the most important next step. CSK data can be obtained through the ASI scientific data program (helpdesk@cosmo-skymed.it) or ESA Third Party Missions (https://eoregistration.esa.int/). A stack of 15-20 X-band Spotlight SLC images would test whether multi-pass processing with real baselines produces tomographic features at the claimed depths. Note that under standard SAR physics, X-band cannot penetrate limestone to reach the internal chambers; any detected features at 20-80 m depth would therefore constitute evidence for the phononic mechanism, while absence of features would be consistent with standard electromagnetic theory.
- Our 15-image Sentinel-1 C-band multi-pass experiment (Section 6.4) establishes a negative baseline: neither C-band resolution (64 m) nor C-band penetration depth is sufficient to detect pyramid internal structures.
- The Gran Sasso validation claim (1.4 km depth) should be independently verified, as it represents the strongest testable prediction of the method and one that neither standard SAR tomography nor the phononic mechanism can achieve with available satellite configurations.

## 8. Reproducibility

All code used in this analysis is available at https://github.com/simossss/sar-tomography-reproduction. The analysis requires only freely available data and open-source software:

- Umbra SICD data: AWS Open Data Program, s3://umbra-open-data-catalog/
- Sentinel-1 SLC data: Alaska Satellite Facility (https://search.asf.alaska.edu/), Track 58 Ascending, 2023
- Analysis code: Python 3.12, sarpy, numpy, scipy
- Scripts:
  - `verify_v2.py` — Extracts SAR metadata and computes kz baseline geometry using corrected Doppler FM rate mapping
  - `fix_coherence.py` — Measures sub-aperture coherence using standard InSAR windowed estimator
  - `sweep_v2.py` — 240-experiment parameter sweep with Giza/desert comparison using corrected geometry
  - `step3_baselines.py` — Approximate multi-pass baseline computation from Sentinel-1 orbit state vectors
  - `run_tomo.py` — Preliminary multi-pass tomographic inversion (simplified processing, see Section 6.4 caveats)
  - `multi_site_tomo.py` — 20-site comparison (simplified processing, see Section 6.4 caveats)
  - `aliasing_simulation.py` — Synthetic PSF illustration of sidelobe contamination with 6 vs 200 images

Total compute time: approximately 15 minutes on a standard workstation.
